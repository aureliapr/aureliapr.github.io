Critical Pedagogy in CS Education

Study Motivation

Why explore the impacts of critical pedagogy in computer science education?

When examining examples of technological design in society, they often cater to a single type of user. Technology continues to perpetuate the matrix of domination. There are well-documented instance of technological design failing people with different abilities, identities, and knowledge. One example of a technology that fails to include all users is the soap dispenser. Many soap sensors fail to recognize deeper skin tones. This is an example of a technological dysfunction that negatively impacts marginalized people in their everyday lives. So why is this happening? Designers are not intentionally trying to exclude users. But they have not been trained to think about inclusive design practices. Changing the way society approaches technological design starts in undergraduate education. By incorporating ethical thinking into computer science education, we can instill a sense of responsibility in upcoming software engineers and designers.

Previous Studies

What were the limitations of previous studies?

Professors have been integrating critical pedagogy into undergraduate computer science education. So far, they have found that the intergration of ethics into a final integrative class at the end of university is not effective. However, the integration throughout students' college education is more effective. One limitation of previous research, however, is that it only provided qualitative evidence for how effective the integration of critical pedagogy was.

Our Study

In our study, author 4 integrated critical pedagogy into a Software Design class in a midwestern liberal arts college. They integrated pedagogy utilizing two frameworks from prior works. The first is the CIDER elicitation technique, which is a framework that helps students analyze assumptions embedded in technology. Throughout the software design class students created a database backed website. After every technical milestone they did a cider analysis of their product. The second way was by making the values embedded in technology explicit in lecture and classwork. In prior iterations of software design, professors lectured about the benefits and drawbacks of a technology. Our iteration of the class went over the benefits and drawbacks, as well as going over who the intended users of a certain technology were, the technologyâ€™s purpose, and the values embedded in that technology.

In addition to qualitative analysis we were among the first to use a validated survey called Crit Con Index to measure changes in attitudes towards critical reflection and agency quanitatively. Our study provides quantitative and qualitative evidence to answer the questions:

  1. To what extent does the integration of critical pedagogy in post-secondary software desing changel students' attitudes towards critical reflection & agency?
  2. How might critical pedagogy in software desing shape students' critical reflection & agency?
  3. Why might critical pedaggoy in software desing shape students' critical reflection & agency?

How did we analyze changes in students' critical reflection & agency?

Methods

Data Sources and Analysis

23 students constented to share their classwork and survey data. 5 students constented to interviews.

01
CritCon Index Pre & Post Survey

The first data source we used was the CritCon Index Survey. The survey has been validated to measure attitudes towards critical reflection and agency in Computer Science. It was administered at the start and end of class. We analyzed the results of the survey with non-parametric statistical tests. This survey helped us understand how much reflection and agency changed over the course of software design.

02
Classwork Data

The second data source we used was studentsâ€™ classwork. We used a method called deductive coding to organize the main themes of students work into broad categories based on a prior framework. The framework was from the CIDER paper. This data helped us understand how critical pedagogy might have shaped students critical reflection and agency.

03
Interview Data

The third data source was interview data.We used inductive coding to analyze the interviews. Inductive coding is similar to deductive coding but the categories do not come from a prior framework. This data helped us understand why critical pedagogy might have shaped students critical reflection and agency.

Significant CritCon Results

Students Personal Effectiveness Increased

We found that after taking software design, students' personal effectiveness increased. The difference between students' pre and post attitudes around personal effectiveness had a large effect size (V = 0, p = 6.388 10^{-5}, r = 0.834). This finding suggests that the concepts and skills learned in the software design course changed students' beliefs in their ability to discuss and act on ethical issues. Before software design students were between slightly disagreeing and agreeing that they had personal effectiveness. After taking software design, they agreed that they had personal effectiveness. This shows improvement in personal effectiveness.

Valuing Marginalized Perspectives Increased

Students valued input from marginalized groups in developing software more after taking software design. The difference between students' pre and post attitudes towards input from marginalized groups had a moderate effect size(V = 18, p = 0.043, r = 0.423). The software design class had a moderate effect on students perceptions of the importance of input from marginalized groups in design. Before software design students were between agreed that including marginalized perspectives was important. After taking software design, they strongly agreed.

Classwork Results

Variety of Assumptions Identified Increased

A potential explanation for how changes in personal effectiveness and valuing marginalized perspectives occurred is that classwork facilitated students' critical reflection. Several classwork assignments asked students to evaluate the assumptions about users embedded in various technology. The diversity of assignments allowed students to learn how to identify a wide variety of assumptions. For example, the first assignment that students completed was analyzing the assumptions, intended users, and values embedded in Unix & Vim. The top three types of assumptions students identified made up more than half of the assumptions. However, in the last assignment, where students used CIDER to analyze their web applications, they identified a wider variety of assumptions â€” 16 different types of assumptions compared to 11 in Unix & Vim.

Critical Agency: Fixes vs Unattempted Fixes

Based on the assumptions students identified, they implemented fixes to their web applications. The top type of fix students implemented on their website were accessibility-related fixes. Students accomplished this through text-to-speech functionality, tab-based navigation or color-blind friendly palettes. The fixes allowed users with visual, motor or auditory constraints to access their website. This was a top fix likely because students were exposed to lots of examples of technology that reflected ability-based assumptions about their users.

The top three fixes, that were not implemented were related to information on their project's database, navigation on the web application, and feature changes. Since the teams came up with more improvements in those categories, they implemented some of them but did not necessarily have time to get to all of them. In addition, feature changes are more difficult to implement than other types of improvements as they require more time, code, and technical knowledge. Students were constrained by our institution's 10-week term and most students take 2 additional classes in addition to this class. This further limited their time.

Through active practice of both critical reflection and agency in their classwork, students might have felt more comfortable acting on ethical issues, perhaps explaining their increased personal effectiveness.

Interview Results

Valuing Marginalized Perspectives From Ideals to Practice

One theme that we found in our interviews is that students' value for marginalized perspectives went from being an ideal to a practice. Throughout the course, students developed their understanding of how to include marginalized perspectives in the design of technology. At the start of the course, they had a general idea of the importance of marginalized perspectives. However, it was not until after software design that they developed concrete strategies to accommodate marginalized groups of people.

In Penguin's interview they explicitly reflected on how the CIDER elicitation technique created a framework for considering the impacts of a technology on marginalized groups. CIDER helped students analyze technological design and develop feasable changes to accomodate marginalized communities. It was a key driver in developing students agency.

Through CIDER students reflected on a variety of impacts from the indivdual to societal scale. See quotes from Womp and Chem which demonstrate the scopes of impact different students analyzed.
